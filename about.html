Research

My methodological research focuses on penalized regression modeling, a popular tool high-dimensional data analysis. I’m specifically interested in inferential approaches for sparse penalized regression models.

In this area my contributions include marginal false discovery rate inferential methods for penalized regression models, many of which are implemented in ncvreg, an R package for fitting penalized regression models developed and maintained by my thesis advisor Patrick Breheny.

A description of these contributions, details on some of the applied research projects I’ve worked on, and some other statistical interests of mine are given in the sections that follow.

Marginal False Discovery Rates

Many penalized regression methods such as LASSO, elastic net, SCAD, and MCP, naturally perform variable selection during the model fitting process. For these models a simple question that an analyst might ask is: “How many of the variables selected by the model are expected to be false discoveries?”

Marginal false discovery rates provide a very general method for answering this question. The method is currently implemented for linear, logistic, and Cox regression under the LASSO, Elastic Net, SCAD, and MCP penalties in the ncvreg package.

Details can be found in the publication:

  • Marginal false discovery rate control for likelihood-based penalized regression models Miller R and Breheny P Biometrical Journal, 2019. link

Feature Specific Inference using Local False Discovery Rates

Knowing the overall false discovery rate for the selections made by a model is useful, but more valuable is information on individual variables within the model. To address this goal we develop feature specific inference for penalized regression models using local false discovery rates.

The figure above displays modeling results from a single simulated data set containing various types of variables (features). The left panel shows the standard LASSO coefficient path that is returned by default from most standard software packages such as glmnet. From this path it is difficult to distinguish between important features and noise. The cross-validated model, which is indicated by the dotted vertical line, contains several noise variables that cannot be easily identified using just the coefficient path. The right panel displays each feature’s local marginal false discovery rate (mfdr) along the same sequence of models. This approach is capable of clearly distinguishing between important variables and noise; the method characterizes each of the noise variables in the cross-validated model as having a greater than 50% chance of being a false discovery.

The method is currently implemented in the ncvreg package and you can read about it on this page of the ncvreg website.

Details can be found in the paper below (currently in submission):

  • Feature-specific inference for penalized regression models using local false discovery rates Miller R and Breheny P, In Submission arxiv link

Marginal False Discovery Rate Approaches for Group LASSO Models

In many modeling applications the predictor variables are not unrelated entities, but rather are members of structured groups. Some examples include groups of binary indicators representing a single categorical variable, or basis expansions used to capture non-linear effects of a continuous variable. The group LASSO is an extension of the LASSO that is well-suited for these applications and achieves sparsity at the group level rather than the individual variable level. My work in this domain primarily involves group level false discoveries, however I’m generally interested in grouped or hierarchical variable selection approaches.

Iowa Medicaid Expansion

As states sought to expand Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act, Iowa adopted an expansion model based upon personal responsibility. As part of this expansion some recipients were required to pay a monthly premium; however, these premiums would be waived if the member completed Iowa’s Healthy Behaviors Program. Members who did not complete the requisite behaviors and did not pay their monthly premium were disenrolled from the program. The project I’m involved with seeks to understand who is being disenrolled using claims and encounter data. This work has been sponsored by the Iowa Public Policy Center, and is a collaborative effort with Natoshia Askelson, Elizabeth Momany.

Driving Research

I’ve worked as both a research assistant and a statistical consultant on projects involving teen driving utilizing data from Iowa Department of Transportation (IDOT). Some projects I’ve work on include studying factors associated with crash severity among teen drivers and linking licensure and accident reporting records databases. From my work in this area I’m interested in studying multiple imputation approaches for structured datasets, and comparing induced exposure models versus rate-based models for evaluating crash risk factors.

I’m currently collaborating with researchers at the National Advanced Driving Simulator (NADS) on projects in drugged an impaired driving and vehicle to vehicle alert systems. Right now I’m working with 4 summer MAP students in these areas.
